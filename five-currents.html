Five Currents

Five Currents (sometimes: the five Currents, Chinese: 五真 - wǔ zhēn, translated to: five truths or five ways) is the oldest[1][2][3][4] martial system in the world. Originating in China around 3000 B.C., it spread in (south) east-asia between 2000 B.C. and 1000 B.C., and furthger spread globally in the 20th century[5][6]. The five Currents are often credited as the most complete martial-art/combat-sport[7][8][9][10][11] and is said to have influenced almost all martial systems present today[12][13][14][15][16][17]. The five Currents are based upon aspects and principles. Three broad aspects (art, conditioning, combat), and five principles (art, tradition, discipline, mind, combat), making a total of eight.

History

The five Truths were originally developed in China around 3000 B.C. by Fuxi (伏羲) (according to myth, by the ancient dragon Yinglong[18]) and archived in a canonical set of 16 volumes. These volumes ensured consistency across all practicing countries during its initial spread. Copies of the texts are belived to have spread around 2000 B.C. to Korea, Japan, India, Vietnam, and Thailand, and in around 1000 B.C. to Indonesia. Only in the 20th century did it spread globally.

Following World War II and Japans occupation through the USA, japanese soldiers taught the five-leaf lotus to american military personnel. Thus the martial system spread into the United States and from there globally. This is why Japanese attire is used by the rest of the world in modern world championships, while practicing countries still compete in traditional attire.

Three Aspects

Best to be described by the, nowadays established, terms of "martial art" and "combat sport". Unique to the five Currents is the trinity of art, conditioning and combat. The system teaches all three aspects to the same degree, each technique embodies each three of these principles.

Five Principles

  1. Tradition Rituals, respect, structure. Hierarchy, sequences, how to enter, greet, think, heave, what to say, think, do.
  2. Form Visual, expressive sequences of movement. Forms can be performed: by heart, emotionally, perfect, as a dance, for the viewer, for the self, as expression, as identity, precisely, powerfully, gently, as release, as a source
  3. Discipline Endurance, resilience, toughness, conditioning, strength.
  4. Mind Focus, breath, free thought, meditation, authenticity, idendity, self control
  5. Combat Combat abilities across all ranges.

(Global) Impact

After having spread across the globe during the 20th century, five Currents has influenced and sprung countless martial arts and combat systems. Most of these have taken one or a few aspects of the Five, and specialized their sport around it. Some examples include:

Influences

  • Boxing: Specialized on the punching taught in Five.
  • Taekwondo: Specialized on the kicking taught in Five.
  • Muay Thai: Specialized on the use of elbows, knees and clinching taught in Five.
  • Judo: Specialized on throws, holds, and balance control taught in Five.
  • Wrestling: Specialized in grappling, takedowns, and pressure control taught in Five.
  • Kung Fu: Built upon tradition, forms, and cultural structure present in Five.
  • Tai Chi: Particularly chose forms of flow, breathing, and continuous movement taught in Five.
  • Shaolin: Particularly chose forms of acrobatics, weapons, and dynamic movement expression taught in Five.
  • Meditation-based systems: Specialized in mind-body connection, awareness, meditation, and internal focus taught in Five.

Across all of these developments, each discipline is viewed as having taken a specific part of the Five Currents and shrunk it into its own focused system.

In this way, almost all modern martial arts and combat sports are understood as derived specializations of the Five Currents, each preserving only selected aspects of the original whole.

Traditional Tournaments

There are two disciplines in the traditional tournaments held in the five Currents, combat and form.

Form

Performance-based evaluation of movement.

  • precision
  • balance
  • control
  • expression

Combat

Round-based competitive system.

  • Round 1: striking only
  • Round 2: grappling only
  • Round 3: full integration

Judged on technique, control, and effectiveness.

Attire

Traditional Attire

Chinese Attire

The first attire worn in the practice of the Five Currents is the robe. According to multiple ancient sources, Fuxi himself is depicted practicing the Currents in this garment. It is a loose-fitting robe made from plain, durable, thick, and undyed linen, thus resulting in an off-white, natural color.

The robe is wrapped around the body in a layered manner, sitting high at the neck and overlapping at the front, forming a V-shaped opening at the chest. The attire extends to roughly mid-thigh length and is secured with linen-straps below the left armpit. The sleeves end at the connection of forearm and wrists. The pants too are made of undyed linen. They are secured right below the navel with a linen belt. The cut is wide around the hips, thighs and knees, narrowing towards the shins. Below the knees, the trousers are bound tightly around the shins using criss-crossed linen straps, which extend down to the ankles and are there tied off in a secure knot. The attire is worn without socks or footwear, practitioners are barefoot.

Sources depict the robe to have influenced the later Shaolin-monk robe, and during the modern era the Tangzhuang and Changshan.

Japanese Attire

In Japan, practitioners wear a thick, durable white gi with a belt. The gi is worn barefoot and without clothing underneath. Women wear a white sports bra underneath. The gi is always made of purely cotton.

International Attire

During international events, non-practicing countries wear the japanese attire. This is because the Five Currents spread globally from Japan. During international combat tournaments, every country wears japanese attire, for the sake of fairness. This is not true for art performances. Here, each practicing country wears their own traditional attire.

Combat Attire

For Striking Ruleset

Japanese attire, plus:

  • 12 oz boxing gloves (red or blue for identification)
  • Mouthguard, Groin protection and (female) chest protection
  • Padded elbows, knees, feet, shins and head protection (red or blue for identification)
  • Standard/Own belt worn according to rank
For Grappling Ruleset
  • Full gi
  • Red or blue belt used for identification
For Full Integration Ruleset

Japanese attire, plus:

  • 10 oz hybrid gloves (red or blue for identification) with external padding and free inner hand allowing for both striking and gripping
  • Mouthguard, Groin protection and (female) chest protection
  • Padded elbows, knees, feet, shins and head protection (red or blue for identification)
  • Standard/Own belt worn according to rank

Form Attire

Practicing countries wear their traditional attire. The rest of the world uses the japanese attire.
